# Methodology

Three books, on which we lean constantly.

---

## Robert McKee — *Story*

### Controlling Idea

A single sentence in the form: "**Life becomes X when the hero does Y, because Z.**" This is the spine of the film. Every scene must work on it.

If you can't state the controlling idea in one sentence — the film has no spine. Idea first, scenes second.

### Scene value (movement of value)

Inside any scene, **something changes** — from + to – or vice versa. A value enters the scene in one state, exits in another.

**A scene without a value turn is not a scene.** It's just description.

Before writing a scene, answer:

- Which value enters? (e.g., trust)
- In what state? (e.g., the team trusts the leader)
- In what state does it exit? (e.g., the team suspects the leader)
- How does that happen? (Which action flips the value?)

Typical value pairs:

- Control ↔ helplessness
- Trust ↔ betrayal
- Love ↔ obsession
- Life ↔ death
- Freedom ↔ captivity
- Discipline ↔ chaos
- Team ↔ instrument
- Hope ↔ despair
- Knowledge ↔ ignorance

### Inciting Incident

The event that knocks the hero's life out of balance. It must hit **in the first quarter** of the film (for a 90-minute movie — by minute 22 at the latest).

Without this event, the plot doesn't start. The hero's life **before** the inciting incident and **after** — two different worlds.

### Progressive Complications

Each subsequent conflict is harder than the last. The gap between the hero's expectation and the result of his action — widens.

If your third-act conflict is weaker than your first-act conflict — the structure is broken.

### Crisis → Climax → Resolution

- **Crisis:** the hero's last dilemma. A choice between two incompatible values. The hardest point.
- **Climax:** the action that answers the dilemma. The most physical and loudest moment in the film.
- **Resolution:** a brief coordinate of the new world after the climax.

### The pendulum: + and –

The hero can't keep winning, and can't keep losing. Alternation. With each iteration, amplitude grows.

In a tragic-fall arc, the +'s become local (small wins), the –'s become systemic (large losses).
In an ascending arc, the inverse.

### Show, don't tell

Replace interior monologue with action and objects. An object in frame says more than a line.

A pregnancy test instead of "she's pregnant."
A torn letter instead of "he broke up with her."
An empty chair instead of "he's alone."

## Joseph Campbell — *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*

Use Campbell as **diagnostic**, not dogma. Not every story is a hero's journey in pure form. Especially if you're writing an antihero or a tragedy — there the mirror path applies (*katabasis* with no return, like Orpheus after Eurydice).

### The 12 stages

1. **Ordinary World** — the hero in equilibrium. Show what *is*, so the audience understands what will be lost.
2. **Call to Adventure** — an event that demands the hero leave home.
3. **Refusal of the Call** — the hero hesitates, fears. Without this stage the hero is not a hero — he's a function.
4. **Meeting the Mentor** — someone gives a tool, skill, or knowledge. Can be a *false* mentor = the shadow.
5. **Crossing the Threshold** — the hero crosses the boundary between worlds.
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** — settling into the new world.
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** — preparation for the ordeal.
8. **The Ordeal** — encounter with death or the shadow.
9. **The Reward** — the hero gets what he came for.
10. **The Road Back** — return to the ordinary world (or, in antihero stories — the impossibility of returning).
11. **Resurrection** — the final test. The hero dies and is reborn.
12. **Return with the Elixir** — the hero brings something new to the world.

### Archetypes

- **Hero** — active engine of the plot.
- **Shadow** — antihero, mirror of the hero's dark side.
- **Mentor** — gives the tool.
- **Threshold Guardian** — obstacle at the entrance to the new world.
- **Herald** — announces the call.
- **Shape-shifter** — switches sides.
- **Trickster** — comic figure breaking the rules.

In antiheroic tragedy, **the mentor is often the shadow**. The hero doesn't realize his teacher is his enemy.

## Aristotle — *Poetics*

### Hamartia (ἁμαρτία) — the fatal flaw

Not "sin" in the moral sense, but a flaw in character that triggers tragedy. A good hero has a weak trait that, in ordinary life, is a virtue — and in extraordinary circumstances, a catastrophe.

"I love too much" — that's the hamartia of romantic tragedy.
"I trust everyone" — political tragedy.
"I cannot forgive" — family tragedy.

Hamartia must be **visible from the first scene** and **explode at the climax**.

### Peripeteia (περιπέτεια) — reversal

An action that should have led to X — leads to –X.

Wanting to save, he kills. Wanting to protect, he destroys. Wanting to open, he closes forever.

A strong film has 2-3 peripeteias, escalating in force.

### Anagnorisis (ἀναγνώρισις) — recognition

The moment when the hero (or the audience) understands what was hidden.

The best anagnorisis is the one that **coincides with peripeteia**. Double blow: the hero learns the truth at the exact moment his action reverses on him.

### Catharsis (κάθαρσις) — purgation through fear and pity

The finale must produce in the audience a mixed feeling — sympathy for the hero plus fear for oneself ("I could have been him").

### Unity of action

Aristotle requires that everything in the tragedy lead to a single point. Every action, every object, every line must work toward the final point.

**If a scene doesn't work on the final point — it's superfluous.** Simple test: remove the scene. Does the film change? If no — cut.

## How to use

Before writing each scene:

1. Which controlling idea (McKee)?
2. Which value enters this scene, which value exits?
3. Which Campbell stage? (If applicable.)
4. How does the scene work on the hero's hamartia?
5. Is this a peripeteia scene or a setup scene?

If the answers are blank — either the scene is unnecessary, or you need to rethink it.
