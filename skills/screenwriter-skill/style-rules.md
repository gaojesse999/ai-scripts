# Writing rules

## General rules

1. **Action verbs. No description.**
   The camera films only what can be seen and heard. No "he feels," "he understands," "memories rush through him," "the atmosphere grows tense." Only verbs (HITS, EXITS, GRABS, LOOKS, WHISPERS).

2. **Brevity = talent.** If you can say it in two words — say it in two. An extra line is an extra minute of film.

3. **Change only what they ask for.** Surgical edit. No "improving" neighboring lines.

4. **Hollywood format.**
   - Slug-line: ALL CAPS, bold, left margin
   - Action: left margin, present tense, action verbs only
   - Character cue: ALL CAPS, indent ≈ 2.2" (3168 DXA)
   - Parenthetical: indent ≈ 1.6" (2304 DXA)
   - Dialogue: indent 1" left + 1.5" right (1440 / 2160 DXA)
   - Transition: right margin, ALL CAPS

5. **One version, not five.** When pitching a scene — give one version + an argument. If the user rejects it, ask one narrow binary question and give the next single version.

6. **Slug-line format.** `INT./EXT. — LOCATION — TIME OF DAY`. Time of day is usually: DAWN / DAY / EVENING / NIGHT. The slug-line is **always** in a single language (either English, Chinese, or Russian — chosen at the start of the project).

## Bad vs Good

❌ "Grey dawn paints the mountain peaks pink. An ancient temple looms over the chasm."

✅ "EXT. MOUNTAINS — DAWN. Three dots streak over a snowy ridge."

---

❌ "The hero feels his heart breaking with the pain of loss. His eyes fill with grief."

✅ "Hero LOOKS at the mountain." (What he feels is the actor's job.)

---

❌ "The teacher, with a mysterious smile, speaks in a mysterious voice tinged with ageless wisdom:"

✅ "TEACHER: 'You already know.'"

---

❌ "A brutal, bloody battle ensues, the hero fighting for his life, baring his resolve."

✅ "Hero HITS. Enemy FALLS. Hero TURNS. Second enemy ALREADY BEHIND HIM."

---

## Character voice (speech identity)

Every major character must have a **recognizable voice**. Read a line without the name attached — it should be obvious who's speaking.

Voice parameters:

- **Line length** — short / long.
- **Register** — high / low, literary / colloquial.
- **Slang** — yes / no, what kind.
- **Self-assessment in speech** — does this character justify themselves, admit fault?
- **Silence** — who stays silent more than the others? Silence is also a trait.
- **Patterns** — which phrases recur for this character?

Strong technique: give each character a **unique form of address** to the same other character. One uses the name. Another uses a nickname. A third never addresses them directly.

## Rhythm and pace

- **Fight / chase** — short verb-driven lines. One verb per line. Cutting rhythm.

  ```
  Hero HITS.
  Enemy FALLS.
  Hero PICKS UP the sword.
  ```

- **Dialogue** — short lines, pauses through action ("Hero is silent," not "the hero says nothing because he doesn't know what to say").

- **Descriptive scenes** — also through action, not through state-description.

  ❌ "The atmosphere grew more tense."
  ✅ "The light goes out. Only breathing."

---

## Avoid

- **Interior monologue.** If you can show through action — show.
- **Landscape descriptions longer than one line.** One frame, one line.
- **Mood adjectives** ("anxious," "ominous," "mysterious"). That's the audience's mood, not the screenwriter's.
- **Exposition through dialogue** ("As you remember, we are close relatives, and our mother was killed in 1992"). That's an info dump — a failure.
- **Lines that repeat what's already on screen.** If the hero is crying, no need for "I'm sad."

---

## Maintain

- **Objects as mines.** Every appearance of an object must move the plot. Show a gun in act one — it must fire in act three (Chekhov's rule). Show it twice — by the third time, you must use it.

- **Repeating lines.** The same line, spoken in different contexts, becomes a semantic mine. "I love you" at the start and "I love you" before a murder are different lines.

- **Silence.** A character's silence in the moment when the audience expects a line is the loudest sound in the film.

- **Symmetry.** First scene and last scene should rhyme. Through an object, a location, a line, or an action.
